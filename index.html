The site read

Three things I'd fix first.

Looked at socreamalicious.co from an outside perspective. Three things that would move the needle before anything on the plan below ever ships.

01

Your shop page displays raw code instead of products.

Visit socreamalicious.co/shop/ and the page loads a wall of unrendered WPBakery shortcodes ([vc_row], [nd_options_text], [woo_product_slider id="719"]) rather than a product grid. It looks like the site was originally built with WPBakery Page Builder and later switched to Elementor Pro, but the /shop/ page still holds the old shortcodes and nothing renders them anymore. Result: the primary sales page is a text wall. It's likely why "Shop" is buried in the nav, because the page has been visibly broken for a while.

02

Your product images have AI-generated text on the labels.

Every product mockup on the homepage was generated by Google Whisk. The image filenames literally start with "thumbnail_Whisk_..." AI image generators struggle with rendering readable text, so the pint labels display things like "CHEF INBOSPIREB NIC IL ARTISANN," "CUSF INEPIRED," "ARTIEER ICE CREAM," and "ARTIEAN ICE CROAM" instead of real product copy. For a chef-inspired premium brand at $9.99 per pint, product mockups with fake text on the labels quietly undercut the whole positioning.

03

Your credentials aren't searchable.

Emmy sponsor. Forbes feature. Oprah moment. Today Show appearance. Essence and Black Enterprise. Nationally distributed at Target and Walmart. Not one of these is captured as a dedicated page or blog post on the site. Someone Googling "Black-owned ice cream brands," "Emmy Awards ice cream sponsor," "Chef Liz Rogers Creamalicious," or "Southern ice cream brands" gets everything except your own site. There's also no JSON-LD schema anywhere on the site, so Google and AI answer engines like Perplexity and ChatGPT Search have nothing structured to work with when someone asks about you.

And one thing I tested myself.

I tried to buy a pint. I couldn't.

Every product on socreamalicious.co is marked "Out of stock" with no purchase option, no price visible on the detail page, no expected restock date. Whether that's a temporary hold on DTC orders or a WooCommerce inventory misconfiguration, the effect is the same: your online storefront can't accept an order right now. There's no messaging explaining why. Every visitor who arrived expecting to buy directly is bouncing.

The bigger question

The platform underneath the fixes.

Every finding above can be fixed on WooCommerce. Every one is also easier to prevent on Shopify.

Here's the honest tradeoff.

Where you are: WooCommerce + WordPress + Elementor Pro

  • Content-first stack, ecommerce added on. Common for brands that started as blogs or informational sites and layered a store on top.
  • Highly customizable, but you manage the hosting, security updates, plugin conflicts, and PCI compliance.
  • The /shop/ page showing raw code (Finding 01) happens because the plugin building the page (WPBakery) was replaced with a different one (Elementor Pro) and nobody caught the residue.
  • Analytics install is manual and can break with plugin updates. Schema is manual (Yoast or Rank Math + config). Klaviyo integration is a plugin that can break.
  • Every WordPress site is one plugin conflict from an outage.

Where a food DTC brand at your scale should be: Shopify

  • Purpose-built ecommerce, managed by Shopify. They handle hosting, security, PCI, plugin conflicts, updates.
  • Analytics install is native and one-click. Product schema generates automatically. Klaviyo integrates in two clicks and stays integrated.
  • Every DTC food brand at Creamalicious's tier (Van Leeuwen, Salt & Straw, Halo Top) runs on Shopify or similar. The ecosystem is built for you.
  • Editorial control on par with WordPress via Shopify's blog and page builder.
  • The class of problems in the audit above cannot happen on Shopify. Plugins can't conflict because there are no plugins in the WordPress sense.

My recommendation: migrate to Shopify. The ninety days does the migration, not just the surface fixes. You get a foundation where the problems in the site read above cannot happen, priced the same as the fix-in-place option.

If you'd rather stay on WooCommerce, the plan adapts. Same ninety days, same price, different scope. That's a decision we'd make together in the first call.
