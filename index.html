The site read

Three things I'd fix first.

Looked at socreamalicious.co from an outside perspective. Three things that would move the needle before anything on the plan below ever ships.

01

You have two shop pages. One of them is broken.

Visit socreamalicious.co/shop/ and the page is a wall of code that never got fixed. Someone worked around it by making a new page at socreamalicious.co/shop-7/ that actually works and shows your products. The "7" in the URL tells me people have tried six times to get the shop page working. Meanwhile the broken one is still live for anyone who lands on it.

02

Your product photos have fake text on the labels.

Every pint image on your homepage was generated by AI (Google's Whisk tool). AI has trouble drawing readable text on labels, so your pints display things like "CHEF INBOSPIREB," "CUSF INEPIRED," "ARTIEER ICE CREAM," and "ARTIEAN ICE CROAM" instead of the real product copy. Real pints don't say that. For a $9.99 chef-inspired brand, having product mockups with fake text on the labels quietly undercuts the whole positioning.

03

There's no cart button on your site.

Even after finding a real product page and clicking "Add To Cart," there's no cart icon anywhere in your navigation. There's no way for a visitor to see what they've added, no way to review their order, no way to get to checkout. Buying online right now requires the customer to guess the URL of the cart page or to know exactly what they want and click one product at a time. Real revenue leaking every day this stays broken.

04

Google doesn't know about your credentials.

Emmy sponsor. Forbes feature. Oprah moment. Today Show appearance. Essence and Black Enterprise. Distribution at Target and Walmart. Not one of these has a dedicated page or blog post on your site. Someone Googling "Black-owned ice cream brands," "Emmy Awards ice cream sponsor," "Chef Liz Rogers Creamalicious," or "Southern ice cream brands" finds every website except yours. Six focused blog posts and some behind-the-scenes code fixes 80% of this and starts sending Google traffic your way.

The bigger question

You're on the wrong platform.

Every problem in the site read above can be fixed on your current setup. But most of them wouldn't happen at all on the platform you should actually be on.

Here's the honest tradeoff.

What you have now: WordPress

  • WordPress is designed for blogs and content sites. Someone added a shopping cart on top of it later.
  • Because it's built out of many separate tools stacked together, the tools can fight each other. That's why your shop page broke: two different page builders got in each other's way and nobody noticed.
  • You (or someone on your team) has to manage security updates, credit card safety, and tool conflicts. If any of that gets missed, the site can break or go offline.
  • Setting up email automations, product search results, and analytics requires manually installing and configuring many small tools that can break with updates.

Where a brand at your tier should be: Shopify

  • Shopify is designed for online stores from day one. It's what Van Leeuwen, Halo Top, Salt & Straw, and most premium ice cream brands run on.
  • Shopify handles all the behind-the-scenes work automatically: security, credit card safety, updates, backups. Nothing to manage on your end.
  • Your email tool connects with one click. Google recognizes your products automatically. Your shop page cannot break the way yours currently is because there are no fighting tools underneath.
  • Editorial control is just as good as WordPress. You can still publish blog posts, edit pages, add photos, all yourself.
  • The problems in the site read cannot happen on Shopify.

My recommendation: move Creamalicious to Shopify. The ninety days does the whole move, not just the surface fixes. Same price as the fix-in-place option, better foundation, better return on the work.

If you'd rather stay where you are, the plan adapts. Same ninety days, same price, different scope. That decision is the first thing we'd talk through on a call.

Month 1

Prep for the move.

Set up a private practice version of the new Shopify store

All the building happens on a Shopify practice store nobody can see except us. Your current live website keeps running exactly as it is. Nothing your customers see changes until launch day. Think of it as building the new store next door to the one you're operating out of.

Set up the automated email tool and connect it to the new store

Klaviyo is the email tool most premium DTC brands use. It sends welcome emails to new signups, order confirmations after purchases, reminders about items in cart, and follow-ups after delivery. All automatic. Connects to your new store in about two clicks. Also gets the sending set up so emails come from hello@yourdomain and don't land in spam.

Install the tools that count who visits your site

Right now your site is a black box. No way to know how many people visit, where they came from, what they clicked, what they bought. I install the tools (Google Analytics, Meta's tracking pixel) that count everything so every future decision is based on real numbers, and so you can retarget visitors with Instagram ads later if you want to.

Snapshot the current site's numbers before we change anything

Before we move anything, I write down where things stand today: email list size (if any), how many people are buying, where traffic comes from. That becomes the "before" picture. In Month 3, we compare directly to prove the new site moved the numbers in the right direction.

Review every pint and every page to prep for the move

Every flavor's page reviewed: description, image, price, ingredients, category. Cleanup opportunities flagged (like the products still labeled "COMING SOON JUNE 2026" past that date). Then every other page on the current site cataloged so nothing gets lost when we switch.

Plan the real product photography swap

The AI-generated pint photos have to go. If Creamalicious already has real product photography from press features or the Emmy sponsorship, we use that. If any flavor is missing photos, we plan one product shoot day to cover the gaps. Real pints, real labels, no more fake AI text.

Pick and customize a Shopify template

Start from a Shopify template (some free, some a one-time $200-$400 purchase) that fits Creamalicious's warm chef-inspired look. Not custom-built from scratch. Faster to launch, cheaper to iterate, and easier for your team to maintain later.

Every page designed and built in the new Shopify store: home, Our Story, all product pages, blog structure, contact, shipping, store locator. Consistent brand voice, warm palette, chef-inspired imagery. Mobile-first (most people who buy food online do it from their phone).

All 9 flavors moved over with the search-engine code built in

Every flavor gets a full product page: name, description, ingredients, size, allergens, real photos. Swap the AI-generated pint photos for real ones

Every product page shows real Creamalicious pints. Every homepage image shows real ice cream. Every link preview when someone shares a Creamalicious URL on iMessage or Instagram shows real photography. No more "CHEF INBOSPIREB" anywhere.

Write and set up the welcome email series

When someone signs up for your email list, a series of automated emails goes out over the following weeks: brand story from Chef Liz, first-purchase discount code (delivered by email, not just shown on the screen), flavor recommendations, soft nudge to try their first pint. All emails written, designed, and tested with fake subscribers before you review and approve.

Write and set up the after-purchase email series

After someone orders, another series of automated emails goes out: order confirmation with brand story, care and storage tips at day 2, flavor pairing idea at day 5, review request at day 14, matching flavor suggestion at day 30. Every touchpoint keeps Creamalicious in their kitchen and drives the second purchase.

Add the preview text and images for shared links

Right now when someone shares a Creamalicious link on iMessage, WhatsApp, Instagram Stories, LinkedIn, or Facebook, the preview is just a URL with no photo or headline. I add the preview text and images so every shared link shows a real Creamalicious pint and headline that makes people want to click.

Move all your non-product pages over

Our Story, About, Contact, shipping info, all rebuilt cleanly in the new Shopify store. Chef Liz's founder story gets the anchor treatment it deserves. Shipping and returns policies in one clear place instead of scattered.

Move the store locator over

Your existing store finder moves into Shopify. Choice of Shopify's built-in store finder (free) or a paid app for search-by-ZIP with radius filters. Retail buyers looking for a Creamalicious pint at Target or Walmart find the nearest location without leaving your site.

Set up the blog

Blog structure ready for the first 6 focused posts (Chef Liz story, flavor origin stories, press wrap). Every post template already includes the invisible search-engine code and social share previews built in, so publishing is one click.

Send you a private preview link to review the entire new site

A private URL where you and your team can click through every page of the new site before it goes live. Review, give feedback, iterate. Nothing switches until you say go.

Month 3

Launch, measure, hand off.

Flip the switch on launch day

On launch day, your website address (socreamalicious.co) now points at the new Shopify store instead of the old one. Downtime measured in minutes, not hours. Old site archived (not deleted) as a fallback in case anything unexpected happens.

Set up signposts so Google keeps finding your pages

Every URL on the old site gets an invisible signpost pointing at the equivalent page on the new site. Google keeps ranking you the same way. Press articles with old links still work. Bookmarks still work. No drop in search rankings after the move.

Every checkout flow tested. Every form tested. Traffic tracking working. Emails firing correctly. Any launch-day glitch caught and fixed within hours, not days.

Tell Google about the new site so it starts indexing
