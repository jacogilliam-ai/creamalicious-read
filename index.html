The site read

Three things I'd fix first.

Looked at socreamalicious.co from an outside perspective. Three things that would move the needle before anything on the plan below ever ships.

01

Your shop page displays raw code instead of products.

Visit socreamalicious.co/shop/ and the page loads a wall of unrendered WPBakery shortcodes ([vc_row], [nd_options_text], [woo_product_slider id="719"]) rather than a product grid. It looks like the site was originally built with WPBakery Page Builder and later switched to Elementor Pro, but the /shop/ page still holds the old shortcodes and nothing renders them anymore. Result: your primary sales page is a text wall. It's likely why "Shop" is buried in the nav, because the page has been visibly broken for a while. Fixable in an afternoon.

02

Your product images have AI-generated text on the labels.

Every product mockup on the homepage was generated by Google Whisk. The image filenames literally start with "thumbnail_Whisk_..." AI image generators struggle with rendering readable text, so the pint labels display things like "CHEF INBOSPIREB NIC IL ARTISANN," "CUSF INEPIRED," "ARTIEER ICE CREAM," and "ARTIEAN ICE CROAM" instead of real product copy. For a chef-inspired premium brand at $9.99 per pint, having product mockups with fake text on the labels quietly undercuts the whole positioning. Real product photography would fix this in one shoot day.

03

Your credentials aren't searchable.

Emmy sponsor. Forbes feature. Oprah moment. Today Show appearance. Essence and Black Enterprise. Nationally distributed at Target and Walmart. Not one of these is captured as a dedicated page or blog post on the site. Someone Googling "Black-owned ice cream brands," "Emmy Awards ice cream sponsor," "Chef Liz Rogers Creamalicious," or "Southern ice cream brands" gets everything except your own site. There's also no JSON-LD schema anywhere on the site, so Google and AI answer engines like Perplexity and ChatGPT Search have nothing structured to work with when someone asks about you. Six foundational blog posts and proper schema fixes 80% of this.

And one thing I tested myself.

I tried to buy a pint. I couldn't.

Every product I clicked on socreamalicious.co is marked "Out of stock" with no purchase option, no price visible on the detail page, no expected restock date. Whether that's a temporary hold on DTC orders or a WooCommerce inventory misconfiguration, the effect is the same: your online storefront can't accept an order right now. There's no messaging explaining why. Every visitor who arrived expecting to buy directly is bouncing.

First 90 days

A rough map, not a promise.

Three phases. Phase 1 is site infrastructure, analytics, and email flows, run on contract. You measure the work. Phase 2 is content, credibility, and capture. Phase 3 is measure and expand.

Month 1 is a build month. All work happens on a WordPress staging environment and a staged Klaviyo account. Nothing goes live until your team signs off. Month 2 is publish and expand. Month 3 is measure and prove what's working. Tap any item below for the detail.

Month 1

Foundation.

WooCommerce theme duplicated into a staging environment

Your current live site keeps running while I work. Nothing customers see changes until you're ready to publish. Same idea as building a new house next door to the one you're living in.

Analytics installed site-wide: GA4, Meta Pixel, Klaviyo tracking

Right now the site has zero analytics. No way to know where visitors come from, no way to measure conversion, no way to build retargeting audiences. GA4 for site behavior. Meta Pixel builds audiences even before you spend on paid ads. Klaviyo tracking connects site visits to email subscribers. Every future marketing effort becomes measurable the day this ships.

Klaviyo set up, connected to WooCommerce, sending domain authenticated

Klaviyo is the email platform behind every DTC food brand at your scale. It integrates natively with WooCommerce so purchases and subscribers sync automatically. DNS records authenticate the sending domain so emails come from hello@socreamalicious.co, not a generic sender that lands in spam.

Baseline metrics captured before anything ships

Before any changes go live, I snapshot site conversion, list size (currently unknown), blog-to-cart, PDP views, and traffic sources. That's the "before" picture. In Month 3, I compare it to the "after" so we can prove the work moved the numbers.

The /shop/ page rebuilt as a functional WooCommerce grid

Currently displays raw WPBakery shortcodes instead of products. Rebuild in Elementor Pro with a proper product grid, category filters, and a working add-to-cart flow. Priority-one because it's the primary sales page.

Homepage editing artifacts removed

There's a note-to-self from the copywriter ("lets delete...") sitting in the live copy. There's an asterisk ("*with love") that's a leftover editing mark. Both cleaned up.

"COMING SOON JUNE 2026" labels updated across products

Three products still carry "COMING SOON JUNE 2026" in their titles. Today is past that date. Either they launched (drop the label), they're delayed (update the date), or the timeline shifted (state the new date). Any of the three is better than the current state.

Inventory and stock status audited across all products

Every product currently shows "Out of stock." Either an intentional pause (which should be communicated to visitors) or a WooCommerce inventory misconfiguration. Audit each product, restore accurate stock status, add a temporary-unavailable message on any product genuinely offline.

JSON-LD structured data on all product, blog, and category pages

Zero structured data on the site currently. Product schema on every pint page, LocalBusiness and Organization schema on the homepage, BreadcrumbList on all URLs. Makes products eligible for Google Shopping surfacing and for AI answer engines to cite the brand as a source.

Meta descriptions and OG tags added site-wide

Home, About, Shop, and Contact pages have zero meta descriptions right now. Every page also has zero Open Graph tags, so when someone shares a Creamalicious URL on iMessage or Instagram, the preview is a bare URL with no image or copy. Fixed with proper meta on every page.

Welcome flow drafted end to end, tested on sample subscribers

Right now signup forms exist but no email fires afterward. A proper welcome flow: brand story from Chef Liz, first-purchase discount, product recommendations by flavor preference, then a soft nurture to encourage repeat. Drafted, tested with sample subscribers, held for your review before publishing.

Month 2

Content, credibility, capture.

Month 1 build published in staged batches as brand review approves

Instead of a single scary launch, we publish batch by batch: shop page first, then meta and schema, then welcome flow. Each batch goes live independently. Lower risk, easier to review, easier to catch anything unexpected.

Real product photography commissioned and swapped in

Replace every AI-generated pint mockup with real product photography. This is one shoot day plus edit time. If Chef Liz's existing shoot library covers the flavors, we use that. If not, one paid product shoot handles it. Every visitor visiting a Creamalicious page starts seeing real pints, not AI approximations.

Six foundational blog posts published

Each post captures a specific search intent and internally links to product pages. Chef Liz Rogers founder story. Origin story for Slap Yo' Momma, Aunt Poonie's, Uncle Charles, and Granma Gigi (four separate posts, each anchoring a flavor to a family memory). Press wrap covering Emmy, Forbes, Oprah, Today, Essence, Black Enterprise. Every post is 800-1500 words, keyword-researched, with proper Article schema.

Press page built with all media features linked out

Dedicated /press/ page that anchors every media appearance in one place. Emmy sponsor, Forbes, Oprah, Today Show, Essence, Black Enterprise. Google indexes this and starts ranking Creamalicious for related brand queries. Journalists writing about Black-owned brands or Southern food brands find this as a canonical source.

Post-purchase flow drafted and staged

After someone orders, they should hear from you again in useful ways. Order confirmation with brand story. Care and storage tips at day two. Recipe or pairing idea at day five. Ask for a review at day fourteen. Suggest matching flavors at day thirty. Every touchpoint reinforces the brand and drives the second purchase.

Wholesale, retail partners, and FAQ pages built

Wholesale page for buyers considering carrying Creamalicious. Retail partners page listing where the product is currently stocked with store locator. FAQ page with FAQPage schema (rich results eligible). Each of these captures a specific visitor intent that currently has no landing on the site.

Reviews platform installed on product pages

Zero reviews on any product right now. Parents and buyers check reviews before trying a new food brand. Install a review platform (Judge.me or Yotpo), set up auto-request emails so every customer gets asked to review after delivery. Reviews accumulate over time and add real social proof to product pages.

Content strategy shift: Chef Liz on-camera

Plan and schedule a batch of content featuring Chef Liz as the on-camera protagonist. In-kitchen shorts. First-person flavor introductions. Family recipe stories. The founder should be the face of every scroll, not adjacent to it.
